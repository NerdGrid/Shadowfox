Air Quality Index (AQI) Analysis — Delhi¶

ShadowFox Data Science Internship — Intermediate Task

Objective¶

Conduct an in-depth analysis of the Air Quality Index (AQI) in Delhi, addressing the specific environmental challenges faced by the city. This notebook defines research questions centered around key pollutants and their behavior, uses statistical analysis and visualization to explore the dynamics of air quality, and computes an India-CPCB-style AQI to interpret the results in terms of public health impact.

Research Questions¶

  1. Pollutant levels: What are the levels of the key pollutants (PM2.5, PM10, NO2, SO2, CO, O3, NH3) recorded in Delhi during the observation period, and how do they compare to national air quality standards?
  2. Diurnal patterns: Does air quality follow a predictable pattern across the hours of the day (e.g. worse during morning/evening traffic hours)?
  3. Pollutant relationships: Which pollutants are most strongly correlated with each other, and which pollutant most frequently drives the overall AQI value?
  4. Health risk: How many hours in the observation window fall into "Poor", "Very Poor" or "Severe" AQI categories, and what does that imply for public health advisories?

Dataset & Scope¶

The dataset (delhiaqi.csv) contains hourly pollutant concentrations for Delhi covering 1 Jan 2023 to 24 Jan 2023. It includes 7 pollutants: CO, NO, NO2, O3, SO2, PM2.5, PM10, and NH3, all in ug/m3.

Note on scope: The provided dataset spans about three and a half weeks in January 2023 from a single monitoring point, so this notebook cannot compare across seasons or across geographic zones of Delhi (both would require a multi-season, multi-station dataset). Instead, the analysis focuses on what the data can answer robustly: pollutant levels, diurnal (hour-of-day) patterns, and inter-pollutant relationships during a winter period -- historically the smog-heaviest time of year for Delhi. This limitation is revisited in the conclusion.

2. Computing the National Air Quality Index (AQI)¶

India's CPCB (Central Pollution Control Board) defines a sub-index for each pollutant using piecewise-linear interpolation between fixed concentration breakpoints, then takes the maximum of the sub-indices as the overall AQI (the "worst pollutant" determines the headline number). CO's breakpoints are defined in mg/m3, so it is converted from ug/m3 first.

CPCB officially averages PM2.5/PM10/NO2/SO2/NH3 over 24 hours and CO/O3 over 8 hours before applying these breakpoints. Since this dataset is hourly, each hourly reading is scored as if it were that averaging-period value -- a simplification made explicitly for this exploratory analysis; it is best read as a relative severity indicator rather than an official regulatory AQI reading.

# CPCB breakpoint tables: (C_low, C_high, I_low, I_high)
BREAKPOINTS = {
    "pm2_5": [(0,30,0,50),(30,60,50,100),(60,90,100,200),(90,120,200,300),
              (120,250,300,400),(250,500,400,500)],
    "pm10":  [(0,50,0,50),(50,100,50,100),(100,250,100,200),(250,350,200,300),
              (350,430,300,400),(430,600,400,500)],
    "no2":   [(0,40,0,50),(40,80,50,100),(80,180,100,200),(180,280,200,300),
              (280,400,300,400),(400,600,400,500)],
    "so2":   [(0,40,0,50),(40,80,50,100),(80,380,100,200),(380,800,200,300),
              (800,1600,300,400),(1600,2000,400,500)],
    "co_mg": [(0,1.0,0,50),(1.0,2.0,50,100),(2.0,10,100,200),(10,17,200,300),
              (17,34,300,400),(34,50,400,500)],
    "o3":    [(0,50,0,50),(50,100,50,100),(100,168,100,200),(168,208,200,300),
              (208,748,300,400),(748,1000,400,500)],
    "nh3":   [(0,200,0,50),(200,400,50,100),(400,800,100,200),(800,1200,200,300),
              (1200,1800,300,400),(1800,2400,400,500)],
}

def sub_index(conc, table):
    if pd.isna(conc):
        return np.nan
    for c_lo, c_hi, i_lo, i_hi in table:
        if c_lo <= conc <= c_hi:
            return ((i_hi - i_lo) / (c_hi - c_lo)) * (conc - c_lo) + i_lo
    # above the highest defined breakpoint -> cap using the last band's slope
    c_lo, c_hi, i_lo, i_hi = table[-1]
    return ((i_hi - i_lo) / (c_hi - c_lo)) * (conc - c_lo) + i_lo

df["co_mg"] = df["co"] / 1000.0  # ug/m3 -> mg/m3

sub_cols = {}
for pollutant, table in BREAKPOINTS.items():
    col = f"SI_{pollutant}"
    df[col] = df[pollutant].apply(lambda c: sub_index(c, table))
    sub_cols[pollutant] = col

df["AQI"] = df[list(sub_cols.values())].max(axis=1)
df["dominant_pollutant"] = df[list(sub_cols.values())].idxmax(axis=1).str.replace("SI_", "")

def categorize(aqi):
    if aqi <= 50: return "Good"
    elif aqi <= 100: return "Satisfactory"
    elif aqi <= 200: return "Moderate"
    elif aqi <= 300: return "Poor"
    elif aqi <= 400: return "Very Poor"
    else: return "Severe"

df["AQI_category"] = df["AQI"].apply(categorize)

# Statistical test: is PM2.5 significantly different between morning and afternoon?
morning = df.loc[df["period"] == "Morning (6-11)", "pm2_5"].dropna()
afternoon = df.loc[df["period"] == "Afternoon (12-16)", "pm2_5"].dropna()

t_stat, p_val = stats.ttest_ind(morning, afternoon, equal_var=False)
print(f"Morning mean PM2.5: {morning.mean():.1f} ug/m3")
print(f"Afternoon mean PM2.5: {afternoon.mean():.1f} ug/m3")
print(f"Welch's t-test: t = {t_stat:.2f}, p = {p_val:.4g}")
if p_val < 0.05:
    print("=> Statistically significant difference (p < 0.05).")
else:
    print("=> No statistically significant difference at the 0.05 level.")

Observation: In this sample, PM2.5/PM10 are actually at their lowest around 7-10 AM and climb through the day to a clear peak in the afternoon / early evening (roughly 12-18h), before easing off overnight. This is the reverse of the "classic" early-morning-inversion narrative often cited for Delhi winter smog, and is a good reminder to let the data speak for itself rather than assume a textbook pattern. A plausible explanation is that this particular January saw daytime activity (traffic, construction, and regional burning) outweighing the overnight inversion effect, but confirming the mechanism would need meteorological data (wind speed, boundary-layer height) that isn't in this dataset. The t-test below confirms the morning-vs-afternoon gap is not just noise.

7. Conclusions & Recommendations¶

Key findings

  1. Pollutant levels: Particulate matter (PM2.5, PM10) was consistently the most severe pollutant relative to CPCB standards, with the AQI frequently in the "Poor" to "Severe" range during this January 2023 sample -- consistent with Delhi's well-documented winter pollution problem.
  2. Diurnal pattern: Pollution is not uniform through the day -- in this sample, PM levels dip during mid-morning (7-10h) and rise to their worst in the afternoon/early evening (12-18h), the opposite of the early-morning-peak pattern often assumed for Delhi. Any causal explanation (traffic, wind, boundary-layer height) would need to be confirmed with meteorological data not present in this dataset.
  3. Pollutant relationships: PM2.5 and PM10 are highly correlated and together are the dominant driver of the overall AQI in almost every hour observed, more so than gaseous pollutants (SO2, CO, O3, NH3, NO2).
  4. Health risk: A substantial share of the observed hours fell into health-relevant "Poor" or worse categories, which under CPCB guidance corresponds to advisories for sensitive groups (children, elderly, people with respiratory or cardiovascular conditions) to reduce prolonged outdoor exertion.

Recommendations for targeted intervention

  • Prioritize particulate matter controls (dust suppression at construction sites, stricter vehicular emission checks, curbing crop-residue burning in the wider NCR region) since PM is the dominant pollutant, rather than spreading resources evenly across all pollutants.
  • Issue time-of-day-aware public health advisories -- e.g. discourage outdoor exercise or school outdoor activities specifically in the afternoon/early-evening window (roughly 12:00-18:00), when PM levels were observed to peak in this sample.
  • Expand monitoring to multiple stations across Delhi and across seasons -- this analysis is limited to one location over ~24 days in winter, so it cannot yet confirm whether the same diurnal pattern or pollutant mix holds in summer/monsoon months or in other parts of the city (e.g. industrial vs. residential zones). A longer-term, multi-station dataset would let a follow-up study directly test seasonal and geographic hypotheses, which were part of the original brief but not answerable with the data provided here.

Limitations¶

  • Single monitoring location -- no spatial/geographical comparison across Delhi possible.
  • ~24 days of hourly data, entirely within January -- no cross-season comparison possible.
  • AQI computed by treating hourly readings as CPCB's official 24-hr/8-hr averaging windows, which is a simplification adopted for this exploratory analysis (see Section 2).
